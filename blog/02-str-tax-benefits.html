If you're earning $500K+ per year and paying 35-40% in taxes, short-term rental properties might be the single most powerful tax reduction strategy available to you — legally.

Here's how it works, with real numbers from real properties.

The Three Pillars of STR Tax Strategy

1. Bonus Depreciation

Under current tax law, you can take bonus depreciation on qualified property improvements in the year they're placed in service. For STR investors, this means you can write off a significant portion of your property's value in Year 1.

Without bonus depreciation, you'd depreciate a residential property over 27.5 years. With it, you can accelerate a huge chunk into Year 1.

2. Cost Segregation Studies

A cost segregation study breaks your property into component parts — appliances (5-year property), fixtures (7-year), land improvements (15-year), and building structure (27.5-year).

By reclassifying components into shorter depreciation schedules, you front-load deductions. Combined with bonus depreciation, this is where the magic happens.

Real Example: $650K Property

Building value (excluding land): ~$520K

Cost segregation reclassifies ~$156K as 5/7/15-year property

With bonus depreciation: ~$156K deduction in Year 1

At 37% tax bracket: $57,720 in tax savings

Add in other depreciation: total Year 1 tax benefit = ~$72,000

3. The STR Loophole (Material Participation)

Here's the part most CPAs miss entirely.

Normally, rental property losses are "passive" — they can only offset passive income, not your W-2 or business income. This limits the value for high earners.

But short-term rentals with an average stay of 7 days or less are NOT classified as rental activities under IRS rules. They're treated as active businesses.

This means: if you materially participate in your STR (and there are multiple ways to qualify), your depreciation losses can offset your W-2, 1099, or business income directly.

Translation: A doctor earning $800K can use STR depreciation to reduce their taxable income by $100K+ — something they absolutely cannot do with traditional rental properties.

How Material Participation Works

You need to meet one of seven IRS tests. The most common for STR investors:

  1. 500+ hours test: You spend 500+ hours on STR activities during the year
  2. Substantially all test: You participate more than anyone else
  3. 100+ hours test: You spend 100+ hours AND more than any other individual

"But I have a full-time job — how do I spend 500 hours on a rental?"

This is where professional management helps. Activities that count include: reviewing financial reports, making strategic decisions, approving maintenance, communicating with your management team, researching markets, and reviewing guest feedback.

With proper documentation, many high-earning professionals meet the material participation threshold while their management company (like us) handles day-to-day operations.

What Your CPA Doesn't Know

Most CPAs are generalists. They handle your W-2, your Schedule C, maybe your traditional rentals. But STR tax strategy is specialized. Common mistakes we see:

  • Missing material participation documentation: Without records, you lose the deduction
